msgstr "The author's work on this book in this direction established that the ancestors of the Tsvets had long lived in the Chernigov region — one of the regions of Little Russia, as Ukraine was then called. For a long time, there has been a version among the descendants of this family that in pre-Petrine times they bore the surname Kolyadinsky, with which their rights to nobility were inextricably linked. However, after the Mazepa rebellion (1908—1709), in which one of M.S. Tsvet's ancestors was involved, his relatives renounced the surname Kolyadinsky and thereby their noble title, taking as their new surname their nickname Tsvet-Samotsvet, and then — simply Tsvet[^28.1]. However, unofficially, the Tsvets were still called Tsvet-Samotsvets for a long time, as evidenced by some items with the initials \"Ts-S\" preserved since ancient times by their descendants."

Meanwhile, the genealogical books list such noble families as the Galagans, Miroviches, and others, who were directly involved in the Mazepa conspiracy but did not change their family name. Tsvet was married twice, and from both marriages, his lineage was continued by seven sons: from the 1st marriage — Pyotr (1814—1864), Yakov (1816—?) and Nikolai (1818—c. 1900), and from the 2nd marriage — Semyon (1829—1900), Miliy (1833—1898), Grigory (1834—?) and Ivan (1836—?). Tsvet bequeathed all his \"undivided capital\" — all movable and immovable property — to his two eldest sons, Pyotr and Yakov, thanks to which they both became merchants of the 1st guild \\[7\\]. Tsvet's father) soon gave most of the money he received to his friend, who was facing ruin (Lyashchenko E.A., 1970, p. 3). Note, by the way, that this friend was Nikolai Aristov, whose wife A.I. msgstr "Of the three elder brothers of Semyon Nikolaevich Tsvet, Pyotr Nikolaevich left the most numerous offspring, who still live in Chernigov today. Of the close descendants of P.N. Although the genealogical roots of the Tsvet family were Ukrainian, the Tsvets themselves considered themselves Russian, without distancing themselves from Ukrainian culture. The lineage of the second heir to the father's capital — Yakov Nikolaevich Tsvet — soon ended: his children left him no grandchildren, and left us no information about themselves. The third brother — Nikolai Nikolaevich Tsvet — turned out to be more prominent in the genealogy of this surname thanks to his publications on beekeeping. In 1856, N.N. Tsvet purchased 10 dessiatines (about 15 hectares) of land near the Belous River, 5 km from Chernigov, created the Nikolaevka farmstead there, and an apiary, which ten years later consisted of a thousand hives and hollow logs. For exemplary beekeeping, their owner was twice awarded a large silver medal at the agricultural exhibitions of 1865 and 1883 in Chernigov, with an announcement about it through the local press \\[8\\]. In 1884, for new hive designs, the Free Economic Society in Petersburg awarded him its highest award — a gold medal. At his own expense, N.N. Tsvet opened an exhibition in Chernigov in September 1886 of the results of his 30-year activity as a beekeeper, presenting 12 types of hives of his own design, including a glass one that allowed observing the life of bees. The great interest in this free exhibition and its exhibits was also noted by the press \\[9\\]."

msgstr "It should be noted that in his inclination towards natural science, I.N. Tsvet (the father of Ivan, Semyon, and the other Tsvet brothers), and he was given a surname similar to their original, but then renounced surname Kolyadinsky. The activities of S.N. Kolyadin manifested the same multifaceted interests that were characteristic of many representatives of the Tsvet family. msgstr "Semyon Nikolaevich was born in Chernigov on February 1, 1829. According to documents, his parents were the merchant Nikolai Mikhailovich Tsvet and Anastasia Akimovna Tsvet, née Rashevskaya \\[14\\], who belonged to one of the old noble families of Chernigov."
msgstr "S.N. Tsvet received his primary education at the Odessa Richelieu Lyceum — one of the most prestigious educational institutions of its kind in the South of Russia. In terms of the level of education and even the uniform of the lyceum students, it was in many ways similar to an institute. Thus, students of the philosophical and legal departments of the lyceum had not gymnasium stars on their uniforms, but galloons, and besides, from 1833 they wore swords as a sign of student status \\[15, 16\\]. It was on the basis of this lyceum that Novorossiysk (now Odessa) University was created in 1865, with which the main character of our book — M.S. Tsvet — will repeatedly come into contact (see below pp. 73 and 165—171)."
msgstr "Having graduated in May 1845 from the philosophical department of the Odessa Richelieu Lyceum, S.N. Tsvet in August 1845 became a \"self-supporting\" student, i.e., at his own expense, and not at the state's expense, at Kharkov University in the category of mathematical sciences of the 2nd division of the philosophical faculty \\[17, sheet 3\\], but in June 1848, after completing the 2nd year and transferring to the 3rd year, he left it, gravitating more towards economic education. Founded anew in 1805, Kharkov University was a relatively young university compared to some others, for example, Dorpat (now Tartu) University, which, although it received its university status in 1802, continued the traditions of its predecessor, the Academia Gustaviana, which had been operating since 1632. It was at Dorpat University at the physics and mathematics faculty in the category of physical sciences that S.N. Tsvet continued his university studies in September 1848 after transferring from Kharkov University. Among the subjects he studied at the new place of study, there were fewer humanities and more natural science disciplines such as chemistry, physics, geology, mathematics, zoology, botany, etc. Much attention was also paid to economic subjects and active mastery of foreign languages, especially German. Against each subject in his consolidated examination certificate were the grades \"gut\" and the highest \"sehr gut\" (German \"good\" and \"very good\" — E.S.) \\[18, sheets 6—7\\]. Almost all paperwork and teaching at the University was conducted in German, so S.N. Tsvet's candidate work on financial and economic issues was also written in it, written on 15 pages in a small, almost unreadable handwriting with many mathematical formulas and calculations \\[18, sheets 10—18\\]. The author of the work signed as Semen Zwjet (in the university certificate it is written Zwet), and based on the conclusions of the reviewers, \"the reasoning presented by him was found to be fully adequate for its purpose\" \\[17, sheets 15, 16\\]."
msgstr "In September 1851, S.N. msgstr "From the autumn of 1851, S.N. Tsvet improved his knowledge by attending lectures at universities in Germany and Paris \\[19\\] at the law faculties, and \"for the soul\" also attended some lectures at the medical faculty (Lyashchenko E.A., 1970). Upon returning to Russia, he entered the service first in the office of the Governor-General of Novorossiysk and Bessarabia (1852—1854) with the rank of collegiate secretary, and then was transferred to the office of the Chernigov civil governor to manage treasury and passport affairs in his hometown. Semyon Nikolaevich's authority grew especially after successfully conducting a number of financial audits, as a result of which in June 1858 he was transferred to the department of the Ministry of the Imperial Court with assignment to the department of appanages and seconded to the Syzran appanage office to the position of assistant manager \\[20, sheets 200, 200 rev.\\]. In Syzran, he wrote his first economic work, published in 1860 in the Moscow journal \"Sovremennaya Letopis\" and titled exactly the same \"Economic Activity and Legislation\" \\[21\\] as the article by N.G. Chernyshevsky, against which this work was directed with all the sharpness of its polemics."
msgstr "In the late 50s, after the Crimean War (1854—1856), two significant forces of Russian society emerged and then clearly defined themselves — liberals and revolutionary democrats, between whom relations sharply escalated over the issues of abolishing serfdom and the path of further economic and political development of Russia. Due to censorship conditions, the socio-political struggle of these two forces manifested itself mainly in disputes around literary-critical, philosophical, and other topics. It was initiated by the famous Russian writer and literary critic N.G. Chernyshevsky (1828—1889), one of the leaders of the Petersburg journal \"Sovremennik,\" who then became the central figure of the polemic."
msgstr "In connection with the publication at the end of 1857 of the royal rescripts that opened the beginning of the peasant reform, Chernyshevsky used the legal opportunity to highlight the peasant question from revolutionary-democratic positions, while criticizing the entire system of serfdom and showing its economic insolvency. Thus, in \"Sovremennik\" for 1858—1859, a series of his polemical publications appeared, among which was the detailed article \"Economic Activity and Legislation\" (1859, No. 2, pp. 313—368). Under the guise of a dispute about communal agriculture, the author advocated for a revolutionary path of agrarian transformations against the reformist path. He subordinated the demand for the nationalization of landlord landownership to the question of the \"quality of state power,\" in other words — to the question of the preliminary overthrow of the autocracy and the creation of a democratic republic \\[22\\]."
msgstr "It was against this work of Chernyshevsky that many authors unanimously spoke out, including S.N. Tsvet, condemning encroachments on private landownership and the property of landlords. S.N. Tsvet disputed the reliability of the sources used by Chernyshevsky and disagreed with his criticism of the redemption projects proposed by supporters of liberal reforms. S.N. Tsvet spoke very disapprovingly against the allegory \"about straightening the stick\"[^37.1], as a revolutionary call for the forcible expropriation of private property in land and means of production."
msgstr "The appearance in print of S.N. Tsvet's first article was largely facilitated by Nikolai Nikolaevich Tyutchev (1815—1878), who subsequently influenced not only the official but also the personal life of his young friend. A cousin of the famous Russian poet F.I. Tyutchev, N.N. Tyutchev was a very interesting personality. In the 40s, he collaborated in the journal \"Otechestvennye Zapiski,\" was a close friend of V.G. Belinsky (1811—1848), after whose death he took care of his family and left memoirs \"My Acquaintance with Belinsky,\" published only in 1914. Being in close friendship with I.S. Turgenev (1818—1883), N.N. Tyutchev managed the writer's estate in 1851—1853, and then entered the state service in the Department of Appanages as a branch head. During one of his official trips to the provinces, he met S.N. Tsvet in Syzran in 1856, and in April 1860, with his assistance, S.N. Tsvet, as a conscientious and capable worker, was transferred to Petersburg as an official for special assignments at the same department of appanages. The chairman of the Department, M.N. Muravyov, who soon became Count Muravyov-Vilensky (1796—1866), who \"liked\" Tsvet, soon appointed him branch head \\[23\\]."
msgstr "Having moved to Petersburg, S.N. Tsvet began to devote all his free time to literary pursuits. With the assistance of N.N. Tyutchev, he took an active part in the work of the Society for the Relief of Needy Men of Letters and Scientists, widely known later under the name of the Literary Fund. In February 1861, he was elected a member of this Society, \\[24\\] which was still very young. The idea of its creation arose in the late 50s among writers associated with the journal \"Sovremennik.\" Among them were N.A. Nekrasov, I.S. Turgenev, A.N. Ostrovsky, A.K. Tolstoy, I.A. Goncharov, and others. At the first meeting of the Society on November 8, 1859, they all became founding members along with A.N. Maykov, M.E. Saltykov-Shchedrin, N.G. Chernyshevsky, and others. S.N. Tsvet became personally acquainted with many of them and even made friends, in particular, with P.V. Annenkov, K.D. Kavelin, A.P. Markova-Vinogradskaya (formerly Kern), and others. Later, fate would bring S.N. Tsvet together with Victor Hugo, A.I. Herzen, and his son A.A. Herzen. But I.S. Turgenev left a special mark on his life. S.N. Tsvet would dedicate the brochure \"On the History of Russian Nihilism\" (1880) to his novel \"Fathers and Sons,\" which shortly before the death of the remarkable Russian writer would end up in Paris and be preserved in his library[^38.1]. Obviously, in connection with this brochure, a correspondence arose between Turgenev and Tsvet[^38.2], which unfortunately has not survived to our days."
msgstr "Soon, however, S.N. Tsvet was forced to leave his friends and Petersburg. Due to the capriciousness of the head of the department M.N. Muravyov, he was forced in September 1861 to become the secretary to the commander of three corvettes setting off on a round-the-world voyage. The detachment was headed by Admiral A.A.
